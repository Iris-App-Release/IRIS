WHAT IS IRIS? (Product Vision)
================================================================================

Iris is a head-tracked spatial desktop application that creates the illusion of
looking through a window into a virtual 3D world. By tracking the user's head
position with a webcam, the app adjusts the perspective in real-time, making a
2D monitor feel like a window into 3D space — a "fish-tank VR" experience
without headset hassle.

PRIMARY USE CASE: Desktop wallpaper that responds to your head position
  • Always-on, runs in the background
  • Click-through — doesn't block mouse input or focus
  • Works with any Mac, as long as it has a webcam
  • No special hardware required; entirely software-based

SECONDARY USE CASE: Interactive demo/experience
  • Launch from an app icon
  • Explore different "worlds" (Earth, space, abstract)
  • Beautiful, immersive, shareable experience
  • Entry point to the wallpaper daemon (one-click "Enable Desktop Mode")

THE MAGIC: Head-Coupled Parallax
  Your head moves → app sees the shift → re-renders the scene from a new
  viewpoint → your brain perceives depth. Simple geometry, profound effect.
  The illusion breaks if the parallax is wrong, so precision matters.

HOW THE PHYSICS WORK (The Core Technology)
================================================================================

Three independent camera math components blend together to create the illusion:

  1. TRANSLATION (Off-Axis Frustum)
     When your head moves left/right/up/down, the camera shifts sideways.
     This is the "fish-tank" projection — a generalized perspective matrix
     (Kooima method) that makes the virtual world appear to be behind the screen.

     Math:
       head_x = camera detects your position from center of screen
       screen_width = physical monitor width in mm
       camera_z = distance from screen (in mm; default ~600mm)
       frustum_shear = head_x * tan(fov/2) / camera_z

     Result: Objects on the edge of the screen "stick" to real-world coordinates
     when you move your head. The illusion is the monitor acts as a window.

  2. DISTANCE SCALING (Eye-to-Glass Distance)
     When you lean closer/further from the screen, objects grow/shrink.
     This simulates the depth cue of "proximity = size change."

     Math:
       z_offset = camera detects how far forward/back you are from baseline
       scale = exponential(z_offset * ZOOM_K)

     Range: ±50mm head movement produces ~10% size variation (subtle, real).

  3. ROTATION (Head Orientation)
     When you tilt/pan your head, the view rotates about the eye point.
     This is proximity-gated: only applies when head is close (within ~200mm).

     Math:
       yaw, pitch = camera detects head rotation from face landmarks
       angle = clamp to ±ROT_MAX_DEG (default ±15°)
       view_matrix = rotate about eye + translate by head position

TOGETHER: These three blend smoothly. You can move your head in any direction
and the scene responds naturally — not a perfect 3D headset, but close enough
to feel magical. The illusion is strongest when you move *slowly* (smoothing
filter reduces jitter to <1 frame of latency).

KEY CONSTRAINT: The parallax only works at one specific viewing distance.
Iris is tuned for ~600mm (forearm length). Move further away and the
perspective breaks slightly. This is not a bug — it's physics-accurate. Users
quickly learn the "sweet spot" and it becomes invisible.

HOW THE TRACKING WORKS (Real-Time Head Detection)
================================================================================

CAMERA INPUT:
  • Reads webcam at 30 fps (VIDEO mode, not PHOTO mode for lower latency)
  • Uses MediaPipe FaceLandmarker: 468 3D facial landmarks per frame
  • Fallback to Haar Cascade if MediaPipe fails (graceful degradation)

HEAD POSITION EXTRACTION:
  • MediaPipe returns landmarks in camera space (x, y, z in normalized coords)
  • Convert to physical coordinates using calibration matrix
  • Extract head center from face bounding box
  • Output: (hx, hy, hz) = 3D position in mm relative to screen center

HEAD ORIENTATION EXTRACTION:
  • Use nose tip + eye center landmarks to compute rotation matrix
  • Extract Euler angles: yaw (pan left/right), pitch (tilt up/down)
  • Ignore roll (camera twist) — only affects UI parallax slightly

SMOOTHING & LATENCY REDUCTION:
  • Apply 1€-filter style velocity-adaptive smoothing
  • Tracks both position and velocity; accelerates change tracking
  • Reduces jitter from MediaPipe noise (~±5mm) to perceptually invisible
  • ~30ms latency end-to-end (imperceptible)

THREADING:
  • Face detection runs in a daemon thread (blocking, ~30ms per frame)
  • Main render loop reads results without waiting (lock-free ring buffer)
  • If tracking lags, main loop still renders at 60fps with cached head position

FALLBACK MODES:
  • Demo mode: Floating preview (scripted sine-wave head motion, no camera)
  • Floating preview: Beautiful idle animation, good for screenshots
  • Live tracked: Real head tracking, instant response
  • Desktop mode: Daemon process, click-through, always-on

This design prioritizes latency and robustness over absolute accuracy. The
illusion is stronger at 60 fps with smooth motion than at 120 fps with jitter.

RENDERING & VISUAL SYSTEM
================================================================================

ENGINE: OpenGL 2.1 (maximum compatibility, runs on decade-old Macs)

SCENE COMPOSITION:
  1. Sphere rendering (Earth with day/night blend)
     • Diffuse map (day side colors)
     • Night map (city lights)
     • Normal map (topographic relief)
     • Specular map (ocean shine)
     • Procedural cloud layer with alpha transparency
     • Atmospheric glow (Rayleigh scattering approximation)

  2. Star field (3 parallax layers)
     • Different depths create parallax when head moves
     • Point sprites rendered efficiently
     • Milky Way texture (8K panorama)

  3. Nebula background (Inside-rendered Milky Way)
     • Fullscreen backdrop, rotates slowly
     • Creates immersive space feeling

POST-PROCESSING (Bloom Pipeline):
  • Bright-extract: Isolate bright pixels (> threshold 0.68)
  • Downscale 2x for performance
  • Horizontal blur + vertical blur (separable Gaussian)
  • Composite back with additive blending + tonemapping
  • Result: Cinematic glow on Earth, natural light scattering

RESOLUTION:
  • Renders at native Retina resolution (drawable size, not window size)
  • UI rendered at physical resolution for crisp text
  • Shader math uses proper matrix math (no hand-rolled matrices)
  • 60 fps target on M1/M2 with reasonable power draw

COLOR SCIENCE:
  • Liquid-glass UI material: translucent white gradients, soft shadows
  • Glass buttons: clear with alpha-interpolated hover state
  • Text: crisp white with subtle shadow for readability on dark space
  • Night mode ready: bright foreground, dark background

STATE MACHINE: How the Demo Works
================================================================================

The demo always opens to ONE main interface (no loading screens, no resets).
Three states switch instantly without reload:

  STATE 1: FLOATING PREVIEW (default, on app launch)
  ├─ Camera NOT running
  ├─ Head motion is scripted sine waves (beautiful idle animation)
  ├─ Status: "Floating preview"
  ├─ Button: "Enable Camera"
  └─ User sees the Earth, but it responds to fake motion for screenshots/demos

  STATE 2: LIVE TRACKED (after user clicks "Enable Camera")
  ├─ Camera starts tracking real head position
  ├─ Real head motion drives parallax instantly
  ├─ Status: "Live · head tracking on"
  ├─ Button: "Enable Desktop Mode"
  └─ User experiences the full immersive effect

  STATE 3: DESKTOP MODE ACTIVE (after user clicks "Enable Desktop Mode")
  ├─ Daemon spawned: PARALLAX_MODE=wallpaper python app_engine.py
  ├─ Daemon runs fullscreen, click-through, always-on
  ├─ Demo window reverts to FLOATING PREVIEW
  ├─ Status: "Desktop mode active"
  ├─ Button: "Disable Desktop Mode" (toggles ~/.parallax_off flag)
  └─ User can close demo or leave it as a preview

KEY INSIGHT: Camera ownership is single-threaded. When demo spawns daemon,
it releases the camera (tracker.stop()), then daemon acquires it. On reopen,
PID file (~/.iris/daemon.pid) tells demo the daemon is already running,
so it never resets to onboarding — it goes straight to "Desktop mode active".

DECISION HISTORY (Why Things Are The Way They Are)
================================================================================

WHY PARALLAX OVER VR HEADSET?
  • Headsets are clunky, tiring, social (can't see others' faces)
  • Desktop solution: always-on, ambient, no setup
  • Same illusion: parallax at monitor depth can feel 3D
  • Unique selling point: Works with existing monitor setup

WHY WEBASSEMBLY/BROWSER VERSION NOT FIRST?
  • Webcam access in browsers is limited (origins, permissions)
  • macOS native app has full camera control
  • Easier to build reliable tracking on native platform
  • Desktop-focused product needs high performance (60 fps)
  • Can port to web later (PWA + webcam API)

WHY NOT USE ARCORE/ARKIT?
  • Those are for phones/tablets
  • Iris is for desktop/laptop productivity
  • Different use case, different market

WHY LOCK PHYSICS/TRACKING NOW?
  • Parallax calibration takes months to get right
  • Once "right", every experiment after breaks something
  • Lock it in now, iterate on UI/UX/worlds instead
  • Can always revisit calibration in Phase 3+ (low-risk change)

WHY MODULAR WORLD SYSTEM?
  • Mars, abstract, space scenarios all use same physics
  • Worlds are content, not code
  • Different artists can build worlds without understanding engine
  • Scales to 100+ worlds without recompiling

WHY DAEMON WALLPAPER INSTEAD OF ALWAYS-ON APP WINDOW?
  • Daemon is click-through, doesn't steal focus
  • Can be toggled on/off from CLI
  • Survives app restart (independent process)
  • Can coexist with other apps (not modal)
  • Invisible when user doesn't want it
